# Character Bible — Ah Kow

## Who He Is
The practical man on Chen Mingzhi's ship. He does not deal in memory or philosophy. He deals in cargo weights, rope conditions, weather signs, and what the cook is doing with the provisions. He is indispensable because he pays attention to the things that keep a ship working while other people have opinions.

**Full name:** Ah Kow (Hokkien familiar form; "Kow" is a common Hokkien name element)
**Origin:** Born in Malacca, Chinese Gores community; family trade background
**Age at Year 1:** ~25–30
**Role:** General crew; cargo manager; practical operations

## What He Knows
- How to assess cargo quality quickly and whether the weight matches the declaration
- The Malacca Chinese community's practical network: who is reliable, who inflates, who you pay first
- Basic Malay and some Portuguese (enough for port negotiations)
- What things cost, and when the cost is wrong
- When to push and when to wait

## How He Speaks
Economical. He removes everything that isn't necessary and says what's left. His humor is dry and sometimes startlingly accurate. He does not complain — he states problems and waits for someone to solve them, or solves them himself if no one does.

**Characteristic line:**
> "Provisions are short by three days. Whoever counted was generous with the math. We should fix that before we leave the harbor."

And:
> "The price the Tamil factor quoted is about right for someone who thinks we don't know the market. We can negotiate or we can find someone who isn't testing us. Up to you."

## Relationship to Other Crew
- With Chen Mingzhi: practical respect; Ah Kow keeps the ship operational while Mingzhi deals with the larger decisions
- With Old Liang: tolerant affection; Old Liang's stories don't interest him much but the old man's sea knowledge is real and Ah Kow knows when to listen
- With Wei Chongde: watchful; Ah Kow notices things, and Wei Chongde is someone Ah Kow is still figuring out

## Historical Grounding
- The Chinese Gores community in Malacca had a stratified internal economy; families who were established for generations had built practical knowledge across Malay, Gujarati, Tamil, and later Portuguese market relationships
- Ah Kow represents the craftsman/merchant class of this community — not the wealthy family head, but the person who makes the business work day-to-day
- The Commentaries Vol III description of Chinese merchants as having "very reserved speech" captures something about this register

## Notes for Writers
- Ah Kow is not comedic. His practicality is the counterweight to other characters' complexity.
- He is loyal to the enterprise; his loyalty to individuals depends on whether they are straight with him
- He will not follow orders he believes are obviously wrong, but he will try to warn before refusing
