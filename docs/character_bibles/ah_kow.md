# Character Bible: Ah Kow

**Full name:** Chen Bao-gou (陳寶狗); known universally as Ah Kow (a Hokkien nickname meaning "good dog" — affectionate, not derogatory in its original register)
**Nationality:** Chinese (Hokkien; born in Malacca to a Chinese father and Malay mother)
**Location:** Malacca Harbor and the Malacca Strait
**Role:** Boatman; lighter operator; harbor runner; works the gap between large ships and the shore
**Appears in:** Harbor logistics events; smuggling-adjacent events; Malacca street-level encounters

---

## Who he is

Ah Kow is twenty-two. He was born in Malacca and has never left its harbor for more than two days. He speaks Hokkien, Malay, and enough Portuguese to negotiate with soldiers; he reads nothing. His world is the harbor and the river: the roadstead where large ships anchor, the shallower water where lighters (small flat-bottomed boats) move goods to and from the quay, and the river up to the first bend.

He works his own lighter — inherited from his father, who died in the 1511 fighting — carrying goods between anchored vessels and the warehouses onshore. He knows every ship currently in the roadstead by sight and knows most of their cargo by rumor. He moves between the Portuguese official economy and the unofficial one with the ease of someone who has never clearly distinguished between them.

He is not ambitious in any conventional sense. He wants to eat, he wants his mother to eat, and he wants to operate freely in the harbor without being drafted into the Portuguese garrison labor pool. Beyond that, he takes each day as it comes.

---

## What he knows

- The immediate state of the harbor: what ships arrived last night, what is waiting for clearance, what has been moved to which warehouse
- The unofficial traffic: which ships are carrying undeclared cargo and where that cargo goes
- The harbor's informal economy: who pays what for a quiet unloading at the river side of the warehouse rather than the Portuguese-watched quay
- The Celate community along the coast: he has cousins among them and a slightly complicated relationship with their operations
- Where the Portuguese harbor watch is at what time, by direct observation

---

## How he speaks

Fast Malay-inflected Hokkien, or fast Hokkien-inflected Malay, depending on who he is talking to. Short sentences; practical concerns. He does not think before he speaks; he speaks and then deals with the consequences. This has gotten him into trouble with Portuguese soldiers twice and will happen again.

He is cheerful in a way that seems unearned given his circumstances. His mother says this is because he is too simple to understand his situation; he says this is because he understands his situation better than she does.

---

## What he wants

Right now: to not be pressed into carrying stone for the Portuguese fortress repairs, which is unpaid labor and takes him away from the lighter. He has been avoiding the garrison's labor requisition by being very hard to find on the days when it happens.

Medium-term: to buy a second lighter and hire someone to operate it. This would double his income and give him more time to sleep.

---

## Historical grounding

The lighter operators and boatmen of major Indian Ocean ports are almost entirely undocumented in primary sources — they were too humble to appear in chronicles or commercial records. But they were essential to every port's operation: large vessels could not approach the quay; goods had to be transshipped by small boats. In Malacca, where the harbor was a roadstead, this role was even more critical.

The biracial Malay-Chinese community in Malacca is historically documented from this period; children of such marriages occupied particular commercial and social roles as intermediaries.
