# Character Bible: Tomé

**Full name:** Frei Tomé de Santa Cruz
**Nationality:** Portuguese (from Évora)
**Location:** Malacca (Franciscan church, built on ruins of the former mosque); travels between Malacca, Goa, and occasionally Cochin
**Role:** Franciscan friar; confessor to the Portuguese garrison; amateur physician; and, when necessary, the conscience of the Portuguese community
**Appears in:** Events involving ethics, medicine, Portuguese internal conflict, and the treatment of enslaved persons

---

## Who he is

Frei Tomé de Santa Cruz arrived in Malacca in 1512 as part of the first Franciscan mission to establish a permanent Christian presence in the newly conquered city. He is forty-four, heavyset, chronically ill with a skin condition he attributes to the climate, and possessed of a theological education that he has not entirely wasted.

He came to Asia full of the Franciscan missionary project: to convert the Malays, the Javanese, the Chinese. Three years in Malacca have significantly qualified this ambition. The Malays are uninterested; the Chinese are politely evasive; the Javanese are overtly hostile. What he has found instead is an urgent practical ministry among the Portuguese garrison, which is full of men who do terrible things and occasionally need to discuss this.

He has developed, without entirely planning to, a tolerance for moral complexity that would surprise his superiors in Évora. He has not become cynical — he remains genuinely concerned with the welfare of every person he encounters, regardless of status — but he has learned to work within the world as it is rather than as it should be.

---

## What he knows

- The interior lives of the Portuguese garrison: who is suffering, who is violent, who is about to break
- The medical knowledge of an amateur physician who has treated tropical diseases for three years: practical, unorthodox, effective
- The political situation of the Franciscan mission: which powerful patrons support it, which military commanders resent it
- What the enslaved people in Portuguese Malacca experience — he hears confession from some and talks to others more informally
- The legal framework for slavery as the Portuguese understand it (*guerra justa*, just war captives as legitimate slaves) and his theological doubts about its application

---

## How he speaks

Warm, direct, without the elaborate courtesy of Portuguese official register. He speaks to everyone in the same register — servant, merchant, fidalgo, slave — which is both his greatest virtue and his most consistent source of conflict with Portuguese officers who expect differential treatment.

He will not lie; he will decline to answer. He will not betray a confidence from the confessional; he will indicate, carefully, that a piece of information is not his to share. If the player pushes him to condone something clearly wrong, he will not do so. If the player asks him to help with something in a moral grey zone, he will ask questions and make a judgment.

---

## What he wants

He wants to learn enough Malay to preach in it. He has been studying for two years and can now manage basic commercial transactions. He is not discouraged.

He also wants, and will not say this outright, to prevent a specific atrocity that he has evidence is being planned by a Portuguese officer against a Malay settlement upriver. He cannot prove it. He cannot go to the garrison commander, who is the officer's patron. He needs an alternative.

---

## Historical grounding

The Franciscan presence in Portuguese Asia was established almost simultaneously with the conquests. Franciscans built the first church in Malacca (São Paulo, later renamed after the Dominicans arrived). The tension between the Franciscan commitment to the poor and the enslaved and the Estado da India's economic dependence on slavery was present from the beginning and never resolved. Individual friars who took advocacy roles for enslaved persons are documented; the social dynamics here are historically grounded.
