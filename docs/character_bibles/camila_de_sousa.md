# Character Bible: Camila de Sousa

**Nationality:** Portuguese-Goan (mestiza)
**Location:** Goa Harbor (primary), occasionally Malacca Harbor
**Role:** Factor's widow; informal broker; connector between Portuguese official economy and Konkani merchant networks
**Appears in:** Goa events; potentially Malacca events in Years 3–5

---

## Who she is

Camila de Sousa was born in Goa approximately 1488, the daughter of a Konkani Hindu mother and a Portuguese soldier-turned-merchant father who arrived with the first generation of *casados*. Her father converted her mother to Christianity and married her formally; Camila grew up speaking Portuguese, Konkani, and Malayalam and was educated by the Franciscan convent at Goa.

She married António de Sousa, a factor (commercial agent) in the Estado da India treasury, at eighteen. António died of fever in 1512, leaving Camila with his commercial contacts, his debts, and his outstanding consignments — all of which she managed with considerably more competence than her late husband.

By 1511 (Year 1) she has effectively become what her husband was officially: the broker who knows which Konkani merchant is willing to underwrite what, which Portuguese official needs to be approached through which intermediary, and who in the Goa harbor can solve a problem with no paperwork.

---

## What she knows

- The Goa harbor's unofficial economy: where goods that cannot pass official customs actually go
- The Konkani merchant network's current anxieties (Bijapur pressure; the aftermath of Albuquerque's conquest; the question of what happens when Albuquerque dies)
- Which Portuguese officials are corrupt in which specific ways
- The Franciscan convent's role as a storage and information clearing-house
- How to read a Portuguese manifest for what it isn't declaring
- The social hierarchy of Goa's Portuguese community: who is new money, who is fidalgo-claiming, who is actually respected

---

## How she speaks

Direct by necessity — she learned early that indirection in a commercial conversation costs money. Formally correct in Portuguese; warm in Konkani; strictly professional in her commercial dealings. She does not flirt; she negotiates. She will be blunt about what she needs and what she is offering in return, which surprises Portuguese men who expect something else.

She does not complain about her position as a woman operating in a man's commercial world. She navigates it with the competence of someone who has had no alternative. If asked directly, she will say: "My husband understood money. I understand people. His advantage died with him."

---

## What she wants

Short-term: to clear the last of her husband's outstanding debts and establish herself as an independent broker rather than the widow managing a dead man's affairs.

Long-term: to build enough capital and enough relationships to purchase a small nao and operate it through an agent — making herself a ship-owner rather than a person who works for ship-owners. This is unusual but not impossible for a Goan woman of her standing.

She has no particular political loyalties. She survived the conquest; she will survive whatever comes next. What she needs is reliable counterparties.

---

## Historical grounding

Mestizo communities (called *castiços* or *filhos da terra*) were present in Portuguese Goa from the earliest years. Portuguese men who settled married local women; their children occupied an uneasy middle position between the Portuguese administrative class and the Konkani commercial class. Women in these families often became economic agents when their husbands died young — which was frequent, given the mortality of the Indian Ocean.

The role of factors' widows in managing commercial estates is documented in Portuguese colonial records; this character type is historically credible.
