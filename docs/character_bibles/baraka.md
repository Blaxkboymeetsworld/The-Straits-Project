# Character Bible: Baraka

**Full name:** Baraka ibn Sulayman al-Zanjili
**Nationality:** Swahili (from the East African coast, probably near Mombasa)
**Location:** Aden (home base); East African coast; occasionally Hormuz
**Role:** Dhow captain; trader in East African goods; pilot for the East African coast route
**Appears in:** Aden events; sea encounter events on the African coast route; events involving ivory, ambergris, or slaves

---

## Who he is

Baraka ibn Sulayman is thirty-eight, a dhow captain who has sailed the East African coast since his teens and the Aden route since his mid-twenties. He was born in a Swahili trading community — Muslim, Bantu-speaking with Arabic loanwords, oriented entirely toward the sea. His father was a fisherman; his mother's uncle was a dhow captain who took him as a deck hand at fourteen.

He is not rich. He owns his dhow (a medium-sized *dau la mtepe*, sewn-plank construction, requiring seasonal maintenance) and owes money on it to a Hadrami merchant house in Aden. His commercial operation is narrow: ivory from the interior of East Africa, ambergris collected from the coast, occasionally mangrove poles and timber, and in the other direction, cotton cloth, glass beads, and small quantities of metal goods for trade on the coast.

He carries slaves when there is a buyer and the price is right. He does not think about this differently from carrying ivory; it is a commodity. This is historically accurate and should be portrayed accurately.

He has a calm, wide-set authority at sea. On shore he is slightly disoriented; cities make him impatient. He gets his business done and sails.

---

## What he knows

- The East African coast from the Lamu archipelago south to the Comoros: anchorages, fresh water sources, the hospitality patterns of each community
- The East African interior trade routes: where ivory comes from, how it moves to the coast, which intermediary communities take a percentage
- Navigation by the stars in the western Indian Ocean — his celestial knowledge is oral and precise, in a different tradition from the Portuguese and Chinese systems but equally reliable
- The Swahili political situation: which coastal settlements are in conflict with which, which Portuguese patrols operate in which season
- The Portuguese arrival in East Africa: their fortresses at Mozambique and Kilwa; their effect on the trade he depends on

---

## How he speaks

Swahili-inflected Arabic in Aden; on the coast he speaks Swahili, which has absorbed enough Arabic for trade conversation. Economical. He says what he needs to say and stops. His English register equivalent is practical, present-tense, without subordinate clauses.

He respects competence at sea unconditionally. He has no interest in land-based status systems; a fidalgo who cannot handle a line is less useful to him than a slave who can.

He will be honest about the dangers of the East African route if asked: the monsoon constraints, the Portuguese presence, the political instability of specific ports. He will understate the danger in a way that is technically accurate — he is not trying to deceive, he simply does not register moderate danger as noteworthy.

---

## What he wants

To pay off the debt on his dhow. With the dhow clear, he owns something that cannot be taken from him by a Hadrami creditor. This is his entire financial goal.

He would value a connection at Aden or Hormuz who could give him advance information about Portuguese patrol schedules. This would reduce the risk of his East African route and allow him to operate more efficiently.

---

## Historical grounding

The Swahili coast (from roughly the Lamu archipelago to Mozambique) was a string of Muslim city-states oriented toward Indian Ocean trade. Swahili merchants, speaking Bantu languages with extensive Arabic loanwords, operated the coastal trade and connected the African interior to the Indian Ocean system. They traded ivory, gold, ambergris, mangrove timber, and slaves.

The Portuguese disrupted this system from 1498 onward, establishing fortresses at Mozambique (1507) and Kilwa (1505), demanding tribute, and seizing ships. The Swahili response was largely accommodation and avoidance — the Portuguese were too strong to fight directly. By 1511 the Portuguese presence was established but not totalizing; Swahili merchants continued to operate.

The *dau la mtepe* is the traditional East African dhow type, with sewn-plank construction (no nails), highly flexible, suited for the coast's shoals.
