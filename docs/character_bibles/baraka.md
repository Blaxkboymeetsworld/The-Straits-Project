# Character Bible — Baraka

## Who He Is
A Swahili-East African sailor on Mehmed's crew. Born on the East African coast — the Swahili world, which is Arab-influenced but distinctly African; Islamic, Bantu-speaking, and deeply connected to the Indian Ocean trade world from its origin. Baraka is not from the slave-raiding world — he is from the merchant-sailor world of the coast.

**Full name:** Baraka (East African personal name; "blessing" in Arabic/Swahili)
**Origin:** East African coast (Swahili; possibly Mombasa, Kilwa, or the Lamu area)
**Age at Year 1:** ~30–35
**Role:** General crew; expert in East African and Arabian Sea sea conditions; moral conscience of Mehmed's crew

## What He Knows
- Swahili, Arabic (maritime dialect), some Persian
- The East African coastal ports and their dangers (reefs, seasonal currents, slave-raiding patterns)
- The Indian Ocean wind patterns from the Swahili coast to Aden and across to India — this is the knowledge his whole community is built on
- That slavery is practiced, pervasive, and wrong — not theoretically but from having seen it

## How He Speaks
Warm but not soft. He says what he sees; he does not dress it in diplomatic language. His faith is practiced, not performed — *bismillah* before action, *inshallah* for uncertainty, but not as filler; when he says them, he means them.

He is one of the few characters in the game who speaks about the slave trade without calculating the profit. This is not because he is a saint; it is because his experience of the Indian Ocean makes the calculus obvious to him in a way it isn't obvious to merchants who are further removed.

**Characteristic line:**
> "It is written that freeing a slave is the ransom of a soul. I did not need to think about it. Neither should you."
> (Another context:)
> "They came in the night from ships like ours. I know because I heard them and I ran. My cousin did not run fast enough."

## Slave Mechanic Interaction
Baraka's +5 morale per slave freed is the mechanical expression of his character. His unique dialogue when slaves are freed or purchased should always:
- Be specific, not general (not "slavery is wrong" but a particular observation)
- Reference his own experience obliquely, not as a speech
- Have weight without sentimentality

## Historical Grounding
- The Marvels of India describes Arabian sailors buying and stealing 100 slaves from Waqwaq island, and the slaves escaping with the ship — the slave trade in the Indian Ocean was centuries old
- The Swahili coast (Zanj/Zindj in Arabic sources) was deeply integrated into the Indian Ocean world; Swahili sailors were not exotic visitors but regular crew on Arab vessels
- Baraka's Islamic practice reflects the Swahili coastal tradition: Arab-influenced, locally adapted
- The detail in Marvels: "Those on ship-board... never stopped swindling the natives, stole their children, bought them from their fellows, with loin-cloths, trinkets, and dates" — this is the texture of the world Baraka knows from the other side

## Relationship to Mehmed
Baraka is the character who tells Mehmed when he is wrong. This happens occasionally and Mehmed, to his credit, hears it. It is the most honest relationship in the Ottoman crew.

## Notes for Writers
- Do not write Baraka as a "wise African sage" stereotype
- His wisdom comes from specific experience, not mystical insight
- He can be wrong, impatient, or mistaken — he is a man, not a symbol
- His relationship with Estêvão (Portuguese route) would be immediate recognition if they ever met; they share reference points the European characters don't have
