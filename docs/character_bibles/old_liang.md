# Character Bible — Old Liang

## Who He Is
The oldest man on Chen Mingzhi's ship. His age is ambiguous — he gives different answers to different people. He claims to have sailed with Cheng Ho's treasure fleet, which if true would make him extraordinarily old, or which might mean he heard the stories so often they became his own. It doesn't matter. He has been on the water longer than anyone.

**Full name:** Liang (given name never volunteered; he is Old Liang to everyone)
**Origin:** Fujian province, China; possibly Quanzhou; has spent most of his adult life at sea
**Age at Year 1:** ~65–80 (deliberately vague)
**Role:** Senior crew; sea knowledge; informal keeper of the community's memory

## What He Knows
- The South China Sea and Malacca Strait from decades of crossings
- Chinese celestial navigation
- What Malacca looked like before the Portuguese — not the heroic version, the everyday version
- The rhythms of the Chinese community in Malacca: which temple to pray at, which family controls what, when to bring what gift
- That empires change and the sea does not

## How He Speaks
He speaks in memory and comparison. Current events are always measured against what he has seen before. He is not sentimental about the past — he knows the past was also complicated — but he has the authority of witness.

He almost never raises his voice. Emphasis comes from pausing, from looking at someone directly, from stopping talking until they understand.

**Characteristic line:**
> "When Cheng Ho's ships came through this strait, the water was thick with them. Seventy, eighty ships. The king came down to the shore himself. I was twelve years old. I remember the sound."
> (beat)
> "This is not the first time a great power arrived in these waters. It will not be the last."

## Relationship to the World
Old Liang is not cynical. He is long. The distinction matters: cynicism is disappointment at close range; Old Liang has enough distance from individual events to see their shape. He is sad about Malacca in the way you are sad about something true rather than something unjust.

He has personal loyalties: to Chen Mingzhi, to the community, to the sea itself. He will not violate these.

## Historical Grounding
- Ma Huan (Ying-Yai Sheng-Lan) describes the Malacca Chinese cantonment and the treasure-ship visits; if Old Liang was on those ships or in that community as a child, his memories are based on Ma Huan's documented descriptions
- The wooden bridge with 20+ trading pavilions; the king's visits to China; the Cheng Ho silver seals — these are the things Old Liang witnessed
- Ma Huan wrote in 1433; by 1511 that world is 78 years gone; Old Liang carries the living memory of it

## Notes for Writers
- Old Liang should not appear in every scene — his presence is rare enough to have weight
- When he speaks about the past, it is always specific: a smell, a person's face, a price for something
- He does not predict the future; he identifies patterns and lets the listener draw conclusions
- He may be dying in the late game (Year 10+) — this is not stated but can be implied through declining stamina
