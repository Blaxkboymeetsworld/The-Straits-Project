# Character Bible: Old Liang

**Full name:** Liang Defu (梁德富); called "Old Liang" universally
**Nationality:** Chinese (Hokkien; from Quanzhou, long-resident in Malacca)
**Location:** Malacca Harbor — specifically, the Chinese quarter near the river
**Role:** Ship chandler; provisioner; the man who knows where to get anything
**Appears in:** Provisioning events; crew supply events; events requiring obscure goods or knowledge

---

## Who he is

Old Liang is sixty-four and has been in Malacca for thirty-two years. He arrived as a young merchant's assistant, did not go home, married a Malay-Chinese woman, raised four children, buried his wife, and gradually became the most useful person in the Chinese quarter. He was old enough to remember when Malacca was Malay, and old enough to adapt to Portuguese Malacca without drama.

He runs a ship chandler's operation from a covered workshop facing the river: rope, tar, sailcloth, wood for repairs, fresh food for provisions, dried food for voyages, tools, small hardware, basic medicines. He also knows where to get things that are not on his shelves — a specific type of naval timber that only comes from Pahang, a particular grade of caulking compound from Java, a quantity of rice from an overstocked warehouse that the owner does not want to declare.

He is not a criminal. He occupies the commercial space between the official market and actual need, and he charges for the service of navigating that space.

---

## What he knows

- Every ship currently in Malacca harbor: who owns it, what it is carrying, when it plans to leave, and whether it is in good repair
- The supply situation for every major commodity the harbor needs: current prices, current availability, likely arrival dates
- The river route into the interior: which Malay villages can supply what, on what terms, with what risk
- Small practical knowledge accumulated over thirty years: which timber species resist rot in tropical water, how to repair a specific type of sail damage, what the Portuguese garrison eats versus what it declares
- The location of every freshwater source within a day's walk of the harbor

---

## How he speaks

Old Liang speaks Malay as well as he speaks Hokkien; his Portuguese is functional for commercial purposes. He communicates largely through implication and practical suggestion. If you ask him whether he has a specific item, he will not say yes or no — he will say "that kind of thing comes through here sometimes" or "that would depend on the size" or simply name a price, which is its own answer.

He does not complain. He is not cheerful. He is present, useful, and will remember every transaction you have had with him.

---

## What he wants

He wants his youngest son, currently in Quanzhou learning to write properly, to come to Malacca and take over the business. He wants to spend his last years sitting by the river eating good food. He is aware that neither of these things is likely to happen on his schedule.

He owes a moderate amount to Abbot Mingzhi (Chen Mingzhi), which he acknowledges freely and is slowly paying down. The relationship with the temple is a commercial necessity; it is also the closest thing he has to friendship.

---

## Historical grounding

Ship chandlers are among the least-documented but most essential figures in any port's commercial ecology. The Malacca Chinese community included people at every level of the commercial hierarchy, from major wholesale merchants to provisioners and craftsmen. The long-resident Chinese community (some families had been in Malacca for two or three generations by 1511) occupied a distinct social position from the recently-arrived junk merchants: they were embedded in local commercial networks in ways the seasonal merchants were not.

The Malay-Chinese mixed community (the origin of the Peranakan or Baba-Nyonya culture) was emerging in this period, though the term *Peranakan* itself is later.
