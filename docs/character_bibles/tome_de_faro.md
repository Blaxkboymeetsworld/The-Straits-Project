# Character Bible — Tomé de Faro (Portuguese Protagonist)

## Who He Is
The player character for the Portuguese route. A minor *fidalgo* (nobleman) of limited means — enough blood to be respected, not enough land to be comfortable. Came east on the armada that took Malacca, or in the year immediately after. Not a conquistador-hero; a practical man who needs money, wants to matter, and is learning what both cost in this part of the world.

**Full name:** Tomé de Faro
**Origin:** Faro, Algarve, Portugal (southernmost Portugal; historically connected to the Moorish world — his family name carries that ambiguity)
**Age at Year 1:** ~28–32
**Role:** Captain of a small vessel; sometimes trade factor; sometimes soldier; whatever the situation requires

## What He Knows
- Portuguese maritime procedure, navigation, and naval tactics
- The Estado da India's hierarchy and how to work within it
- How to read a weather sign and a contract
- Who in Goa and Malacca owes favors to whom
- Basic Arabic trade vocabulary (enough for port negotiations)
- Knows nothing about Malay social hierarchy except what he's learned by offending it

## What He Doesn't Know
- What the Malay world looked like before he arrived — he knows it secondhand, through the ruins
- That the Chinese community he navigates around has a 60-year history in Malacca that predates Portugal's interest in Asia
- The full extent of Ottoman strategic planning (he knows they exist; he underestimates their reach)

## How He Speaks
Direct. Uses nautical metaphor naturally. When formal (with superiors or in writing), slightly stiff — he has some education but is not a court man. When comfortable, dry humor. When cornered, blunt to the point of tactlessness.

He does not monologue about faith, but Catholic framing slips in: "God willing," "if Christ permits," "the blessed saints gave us fair wind." These are habitual, not devotional.

**Characteristic line:**
> "The harbor master wants a gift. The factor wants a receipt. The captain-general wants a report. I want to know which one to pay first and who will know if I don't pay the others."

## Historical Grounding
- Portuguese fidalgos of the period often came east with debt and ambition; wealth from the spice trade was theoretically available but practically elusive
- Albuquerque's policy of mixing races and building permanent settlement (450 married men in Goa) provides the social context Tomé navigates
- Portuguese brutality at Malacca (kill all Malays found) was real policy; Tomé's complicity or refusal is a player choice
- The *degredado* tradition (convicts used as envoys/spies) means Tomé occasionally works alongside men who have very little to lose

## Opening Scene Context
"A Woman on My Ship" — Camila de Sousa arrives as crew. Tomé's 4 choices at the start define his relationship with her and signal his character to the player. The choices should feel like real decisions, not virtue-signaling options.
