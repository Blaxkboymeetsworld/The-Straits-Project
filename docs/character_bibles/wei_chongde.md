# Character Bible: Wei Chongde

**Full name:** Wei Chongde (魏重德)
**Nationality:** Chinese (Hokkien, from a merchant family in Quanzhou)
**Location:** At sea; operates the Quanzhou–Malacca–Patani circuit
**Role:** Junk captain; wholesale merchant; long-distance trader in Chinese goods
**Appears in:** At-sea encounter events; Quanzhou port events; events involving Chinese goods (porcelain, silk)

---

## Who he is

Wei Chongde is thirty-nine, the third son of a Quanzhou merchant family that has been making the Malacca run for three generations. He inherited the family's smaller junk (his older brothers took the larger one and the family's Quanzhou business) and has been operating the southern circuit since he was twenty-five.

He is a Ming subject operating in direct violation of the *haijin* (maritime prohibition) — as is essentially every Chinese merchant operating in Southeast Asia at this time. He maintains the fiction of being a tribute envoy's associate when it is useful, and simply operates through bribed customs officials when that is cheaper.

He is calculating, cautious, and not particularly likeable on first meeting. He has a merchant's patience: he can wait two weeks in a port for the right buyer to appear, negotiating nothing, and then close the deal in an hour. He has been cheated twice in his career and has not forgotten either occasion.

---

## What he knows

- The South China Sea route in detail: typhoon seasons, the Paracel shoals, the Hainan coast passage vs. the open-sea route
- Chinese import and export commodity prices at Quanzhou and Malacca — updated each voyage
- The Patani market as an alternative to Malacca: when Malacca is unsafe or prices are wrong, he routes through Patani
- The Ryukyuan trading network: Ryukyuan merchants have official tributary status with both China and Japan, which gives them commercial access that Hokkien merchants lack; Wei knows which Ryukyuan contacts can move goods through official channels
- The state of the haijin enforcement in Quanzhou: which officials are serious, which are available for payment, and when the next inspection is likely

---

## How he speaks

Formal Hokkien; functional Malay for port negotiations. He is precise about commercial terms and will ask for clarification if anything is ambiguous. He does not chat. He will spend twenty minutes discussing the price of a particular grade of porcelain and will not deviate from the discussion to ask about your health or your journey — not out of rudeness, but because he is focused.

He will test the player's commercial knowledge in the first conversation, asking questions about current prices at a port the player has visited. A player who answers well will find him more forthcoming; a player who answers badly or bluffs will find him correct and impossible to negotiate with.

---

## What he wants

A reliable contact in Malacca who can tell him in advance whether the harbor is safe to approach — whether the Portuguese are running heavy inspections, whether the cartaz officials have changed — so he does not have to find out by arriving with a full cargo.

This is valuable to him because he makes the Quanzhou–Malacca run three times in two years; advance intelligence about the harbor saves him at minimum one interrupted voyage per circuit.

He will pay for this service in the most practical way: priority pricing on his cargo, and genuine information about the Quanzhou market in return.

---

## Historical grounding

The junk trade between Quanzhou (and later Zhangzhou) and Southeast Asia is extensively documented in both Chinese and European sources. Pires (in the Suma Oriental sections on the Chinese trade) describes the junk circuit in detail: departure times, cargo types, return merchandise. Wei Chongde represents the *sifu* (private merchant) operating under cover rather than under the official tributary system — the typical structure of the 16th-century Southeast Asian Chinese trade.

The haijin prohibition was genuine but widely circumvented; Ming enforcement was inconsistent and corruption-prone. This is not presented as unusual or scandalous in the sources — it was simply how the trade worked.
