# Source Synthesis — The Straits Project

All 7 sources are .txt full-text extractions from Internet Archive editions. Read in full for Pass 2 (2026-03-30). This document is the canonical record of what each source contains, its reliability, and how conflicts are resolved.

---

## Source 1 — Suma Oriental of Tomé Pires (Vol I)

**File:** `The Suma oriental of Tomé Pires.txt`
**Length:** ~819,000 chars
**Author:** Tomé Pires, Portuguese apothecary; eyewitness in Malacca 1512–1515
**Edition:** Hakluyt Society, translated Armando Cortesão
**Coverage:** Egypt → Moluccas (Vol I only); **Malacca chapter is in Vol II — ABSENT from sources/**
**Dates:** Written 1512–1515; covers trade as of Portuguese arrival

**What it contains:**

- **Ormuz/Hormuz:** Island with no water (all cisterns); pearl fisheries from Bahrain; horse trade — best Arabian horses 500–700 xerafins; Ormuz as key to all Persia trade; Ormuz merchants go by land to Hormuz, then sea to India
- **Goa:** Horse trade hub — Arabia → Persia → Ormuz → Goa → Deccan/Narsinga kingdoms; armada-building center (abundant wood, skilled craftsmen); Goa described as prosperous acquisition
- **Malabar/Calicut:** 150,000 Nayar warriors; Brahman caste hierarchy detailed; 20,000 bahars pepper per year; Calicut as chief trade center; commodities — coconuts, areca, coir, ginger, cardamom; ports list (Calicut, Cannanore, Cochin, Quilon); Zamorin as dominant ruler; Nayars as warrior caste (obliged to be ever-ready for war)
- **Java/Bantam:** Bantam "almost the most important" port of Sunda; Calapa (Batavia) as Sunda's best port; Gresik as "jewel of Java in trading ports" — Gujaratis, Bengalis, Siamese, Chinese all traded there; rice/pepper/slaves from Java; Pate Unus attack on Malacca with ~100 ships described; Demak's rise after Majapahit collapse; Javanese junks burned in Malacca attack
- **China:** Canton (Guangzhou) as main foreign trade port; Malacca junks anchor at Turnon island 20–30 leagues from Canton; Chinese buy pepper above all; exports — raw white silk, satins, damask, porcelain, musk, camphor; Chamcheo (near Quanzhou) as silk source; 20% duty on pepper, 10% on other goods; Chinese very reserved with foreigners
- **General trade system:** Commodity flow documented port-by-port; detailed merchant nationality breakdowns for each port

**Reliability:** Very high. Pires was physically present; his observations are firsthand. Vol I is the foundation for trade goods, port populations, and commodity economics throughout the game.

**Gap:** The Malacca chapter (Book VI, page 229+) is in Vol II, not in sources/. Vol III of the Commentaries and Ma Huan cover Malacca directly; Malay Annals covers the conquest from the Malay side.

## Source 2 — Commentaries of Afonso d'Alboquerque, Vol I

**File:** `The Commentaries Of Afonso Dalboque Vol1.txt`
**Length:** ~603,000 chars
**Author:** Afonso d'Alboquerque (son), compiled from letters and official records
**Edition:** Hakluyt Society; 101 chapters
**Coverage:** 1503–1507 (Part I only — Ormuz campaign and early Indian coast operations)
**Dates:** Events of 1503–1507

**What it contains:**

- **Ormuz campaign (main content):**
  - Ormuz fleet: 60 great vessels + 200 galleons; Meri ship from Cambay (1,000 tons); 15,000–20,000 men on shore
  - Battle of Ormuz: Albuquerque fires first broadside, sinks two large ships; night battle with cannon and arrows; Portuguese broadside tactics decisive against larger but gun-poor fleet
  - Tribute negotiation: Albuquerque demands 30,000 xerafins, settles for 15,000/year + 5,000 expenses; treaty written in Arabic on leaf-gold and in Persian on paper
  - Flag ceremony: Albuquerque meets 15-year-old King of Ormuz on the jetty; king in crimson satin petticoat, white cap, golden dagger; formal submission ceremony
- **Albuquerque's character:** Invincible spirit; trusts in Christ's Passion; dismissive of captain objections; pragmatic about tribute amounts; direct speech in letters
- **Diplomatic register:** Letters from Albuquerque to the King of Portugal; Arabic/Persian treaty forms; formal demand language vs. negotiation language

**Reliability:** High as a record of Albuquerque's actions and mindset. Compiled by his son from official correspondence — heroic framing throughout, Portuguese perspective only.

**Gap:** The Malacca conquest (1511) is in Vol III, not Vol I. Vol I ends in 1507.

## Source 3 — Commentaries of Afonso d'Alboquerque, Vol III

**File:** `Commentaries Of Afonso Dalboquerque Vol3.txt`
**Length:** ~829,000 chars
**Author:** Afonso d'Alboquerque (son)
**Edition:** Hakluyt Society; 86 chapters
**Coverage:** Goa conquest (Part II) + Malacca conquest (Part III, Ch XVII–XXX+)
**Dates:** Events of 1510–1512

**What it contains:**

- **Goa post-conquest:** Albuquerque establishes mint; promotes mixed marriages (Portuguese men + Indian women); 450 married Portuguese men in Goa; Ninachatu (Hindu ally) and Utemutaraja (Javanese merchant) appointed governors after Malacca falls
- **Intelligence from Malacca (pre-conquest):** Bendahara learns Goa was taken, releases Ruy de Araujo and Portuguese captives; Ruy de Araujo's letter advising large fleet needed for Malacca conquest; Ninachatu as key Portuguese ally among non-Muslim merchant community
- **Malacca governance and customs (Ch XVII — most important):**
  - 5 governance ranks: Pudricaraja (Viceroy), Bendahara (Treasury/Governor), Lassamane (Admiral), Taimingo (Justice for foreigners), Xabandares (4 merchant-group representatives: China, Java, Cambay, Bengal)
  - Described as "most extensive place in the world" for trade
  - Malays: proud people; skilled with creese (poisoned dagger); wear no yellow (reserved for king); must stand 5–6 paces from king when speaking
  - Legal punishments: spitting on violators, striking breast, hanging, boiling, roasting (fed to "cannibal men from Dam")
  - Chinese Gores (merchant community): arrive January, depart August–September; bring silk/porcelain/camphor/corn/gold cakes; described as "very reserved speech"
  - Lassamane: 80 years old at conquest; withdraws to Singapore after fall
- **Pre-conquest negotiations (Ch XIX–XX):** Albuquerque's ultimatum; Sultan stalls; Ruy de Araujo confirms Gujaratis are the main force driving resistance
- **First assault (Ch XXII — St. James Day attack):**
  - 2 hours before dawn: general confession, then attack on bridge
  - Sultan of Malacca on elephant, son on another elephant
  - Portuguese capture bridge side and mosque side
  - Fire set to city and king's houses; wooden royal pavilion (built on 30 wheels, inlaid with gold) burns
  - Portuguese withdraw at sunset; 50 large bombards captured; ~70 Portuguese wounded; poisoned-arrow victims all die except one (saved by cauterization)
- **Second assault and fall (Ch XXVII):**
  - Junk used as fighting platform over bridge
  - Moors unable to bear fire from Portuguese crow's nest; Portuguese take bridge
  - Moors massacred; city falls
- **Aftermath (Ch XXVIII–XXX):**
  - Albuquerque orders all Malays to be killed wherever found; foreign merchants welcomed back
  - 3,000 pieces of artillery captured (2,000 bronze)
  - Pegu merchants released with safe conduct
  - Utemutaraja (Javanese merchant, 5,000–6,000 slaves) and Ninachatu appointed governors
  - City sacked for 10 days; Sultan flees to Pao/Pahang
  - Sultan Mahmud sends ambassador to China requesting help to recover Malacca

**Reliability:** Very high for Portuguese actions. Heroic framing, but specific details (artillery count, governance ranks, tribute amounts) are consistent with other sources.

## Source 4 — Ying-Yai Sheng-Lan (Overall Survey of Ocean's Shores)

**File:** `Full text of Ying Yai Sheng Lan The Overall Survey of the Oceans Shores.txt`
**Length:** ~1,025,000 chars
**Author:** Ma Huan; Chinese Muslim interpreter on Cheng Ho's voyages
**Edition:** C.F. Beckingham, Hakluyt Society, 1970 (Feng Ch'eng-Chün text)
**Coverage:** Malacca, Java, India, Arabia, East Africa — as observed 1413–1433
**Dates:** Pre-Portuguese; describes Malacca at height of power (~1409–1433)

**What it contains:**

- **Malacca (main game relevance — most detailed section):**
  - Founded by Chinese imperial edict 1409 (Yung-lo, 7th year); Cheng Ho gave king silver seals, hat, girdle, robe; raised from chieftainship to city; freed from Siamese suzerainty
  - Geography: sandy, saltish land; one large river; wooden bridge with 20+ pavilions where all trading takes place
  - King and people: Muslim; king wears fine white cloth headwrap, long blue garment, leather shoes; travels by sedan chair
  - Products: yellow swincense (resin), ebony, ta-ma-erh (dammar resin — used as lamp oil and ship caulking), "flower tin" (cast into blocks, used as currency and trade good)
  - Chinese cantonment: stockaded compound with warehouses/granaries; treasure-ships assembled here; departed 5th moon with south wind
  - Wildlife noted: crocodiles, black tigers (likely clouded leopards or panthers), were-tigers (men who turn into tigers in markets — local legend)
  - King visited China in person with wife and son to present tribute
- **Chinese trade system:** Treasure-ship departures synchronized with monsoon winds; silk/porcelain in, pepper/spices/tin out; Chinese community long-established and semi-permanent

**Reliability:** Very high for pre-conquest Malacca geography and customs. Ma Huan was present. Describes a Malacca ~80 years before the conquest — some institutional detail will differ from 1511, but geography, products, and social structure remain relevant.

## Source 5 — The Malay Annals (Sejarah Melayu)

**File:** `The Malay Annals.txt`
**Length:** ~805,000 chars
**Author:** Traditional Malay court chronicle; C.C. Brown translation of Raffles MS 18
**Edition:** JMBRAS Vol. 25, 1952
**Coverage:** Malacca founding myth through Portuguese conquest and Bintan exile
**Dates:** Covers ~1400–1526 (mythologized); focus on 15th century court history

**What it contains:**

- **Malacca founding myth:** King of Palembang (sea-king's lineage) founds Malacca; name derives from the malacca tree; dynastic legitimacy through supernatural origin (sea-king union)
- **Singapura stories (Ch V):** Badang the strong man (demon-given strength); stone-throwing contest with Kalinga champion; early legendary geography
- **Hang Tuah (82 references — primarily Ch VIII):**
  - Hang Tuah, Hang Jebat, Hang Kesturi, Hang Lekir described as Sultan Mansur Shah's champion warriors
  - Hang Tuah at Majapahit court: his charisma drives women mad; Javanese songs composed about him; described as physically commanding
  - Context: Sultan Mansur Shah's embassy to Majapahit; marriage of Radin Galoh (Majapahit princess)
  - NOTE: The full Hang Tuah narrative epic (Hikayat Hang Tuah) is a separate work not in sources/. The Sejarah Melayu provides the historical/legendary skeleton, not the full epic
- **Fall of Malacca (Ch XXIII — from Malay perspective):**
  - Albuquerque's fleet count differs from Portuguese: 3 carracks, 8 galeasses, 4 long galleys, 15 foysts
  - Sultan Ahmad on elephant "Jituji"; Makhdum Sadar Jahan (religious scholar) rides another elephant alongside
  - Night before battle: nobles read Hikayat Hamzah aloud (refused Muhammad Hanafiah as "too brave for them" — detail showing their demoralization)
  - Sultan Ahmad wounded in palm of hand; showed wound to nobles trying to inspire them to fight
  - 20 leading war-chiefs killed in defense; Bendahara Lubok Batu carried off field; called his men cowards
  - Malacca falls; royal demesne becomes Portuguese fort
  - Sultan Ahmad withdraws to Hulu Muar → Pahang
  - Sultan Ahmad murdered by his own father (Sultan Mahmud) after behaving badly at Bentan
  - Sultan Mahmud reorganizes court at Bentan; names Tun Fatimah's son as successor over Raja Muzaffar Shah
- **Court protocol:** Detailed on dress, address forms, proximity rules; consistent with Commentaries Vol III

**Reliability:** Medium-high for social history; lower for military specifics. The Sejarah Melayu is court legitimacy literature — it preserves oral tradition and real institutional memory but frames events to glorify the Malaccan court lineage. The Malay perspective on the conquest is genuinely different from the Portuguese account and both are valid.

## Source 6 — Da Ásia de João de Barros e de Diogo de Couto

**File:** `Da Asia de João de Barros e de Diogo.txt`
**Length:** ~568,000 chars
**Language:** **Archaic Portuguese** — readable for key facts but not for translation
**Author:** João de Barros (Portuguese court historian); Diogo de Couto edition
**Coverage:** Portuguese Estado da India history; includes Malacca and post-conquest period
**Dates:** Events post-1511; covers Bintan exile period and later

**What it contains:**

- **Malacca founding myth (Ch I — Portuguese version):**
  - King of Palembang (son of sea-king union) comes to coast; names place "Malacca" = "degradado/exile" in their language
  - King succession: Raja Sabu → Manoar → Quacem (first Muslim sultan, converts ~1384) → Manfor Xá → Malafar Xá → Alevidim → Mahamede Xá (the king Albuquerque defeated)
- **Post-conquest Bintan exile:**
  - Sultan Mahmud establishes court at Bintan island (70 leagues east of Malacca, past Singapore strait)
  - Bintan city: on a river; fortified with 300 bronze artillery pieces; stockades with poisoned chevaux-de-frise
  - Pêro Mascarenhas's campaign against Bintan described
- **Estado da India politics:** Portuguese administrative framework; fort-building; relationship with local rulers

**Reliability:** High for post-conquest Portuguese administration. Barros wrote as an official chronicler with access to records, but did not visit Southeast Asia. The archaic Portuguese limits full extraction but key facts are clear.

## Source 7 — The Book of the Marvels of India

**File:** `The Book Of The Marvels Of India.txt`
**Length:** ~221,000 chars
**Author:** Buzurg ibn Shahriyar of Ramhormuz (~943 CE)
**Edition:** Golden Dragon Library; translated L. Marcel Devic from a 9th-century manuscript
**Coverage:** Indian Ocean sailors' tales — NOT a geographic or trade manual
**Dates:** ~900–943 CE; pre-dates game period by ~570 years

**What it contains:**

- Collection of sailors' tall tales from Siraf/Basra ship-captains
- **Sea creatures:** Enormous fish (whale-like, 200+ ells long); giant crabs holding anchor cables; fish ramming ships; pilot fish following vessels for days
- **Waqwaq island (likely Madagascar/East Africa):** Sailors buy/steal 100 slaves; slaves escape with the ship at night — detailed account of early Indian Ocean slave trade
- **Island of women:** Ship blown off course in "sea of Malayou" near China; driven south by uncontrollable wind (Canopus meridian = no return)
- **Roc bird:** Castaways lash themselves to roc's leg; carried from island to inhabited region
- **Siraf, Basra, Aden, Jeddah:** Named as ports and home cities; Siraf is the primary hub for these captains
- **Nakhoda (captain):** Used as title; Arab/Persian seafaring hierarchy implied
- **Sea route knowledge:** Mentions Serendib (Sri Lanka), Zindj (East Africa), Herkend (Bay of Bengal), Qaqala (East Africa?), gold-bearing countries (SE Asia/East Africa)
- **Pepper:** Mentioned 4 times as valued cargo; not described in detail

**Reliability:** This is folklore and literary tradition, not eyewitness record. Events 570 years before the game. Its value is:
1. **Voice:** How Arab/Persian sailors talked about the sea — wonder, fear, practical wisdom
2. **Atmosphere:** Sea encounter flavors; the world beyond the known routes
3. **Lore fragments:** Were-tigers (paralleled in Ma Huan), island peoples, sea monsters — usable as rumors and tavern tales
4. **Slave trade:** Arabian slave-raiding in Waqwaq described in detail; relevant to Baraka's backstory and slave mechanic

## Source Gaps

- **Pires Vol II** (Malacca chapter, pages 229+): Not in sources/. Filled by Commentaries Vol III + Ma Huan.
- **Hikayat Hang Tuah**: Not in sources/. Sejarah Melayu provides 82 references; the full Hang Tuah epic is separate. Any Hang Tuah content in-game should acknowledge this — he is a historical legend, not just folklore.
- **Commentaries Vol II**: Not in sources/. Vol I covers 1503–1507; Vol III covers Goa + Malacca. Vol II presumably covers 1507–1510. No significant gap for game purposes.
- **Arabic/Ottoman primary sources**: No Ottoman-language or Arabic-language sources beyond Buzurg (9th century). Marvels of India provides Arab seafarer voice but not Ottoman-era economics.
