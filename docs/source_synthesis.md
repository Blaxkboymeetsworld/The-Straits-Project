# The Straits Project — Source Synthesis

Six primary sources were read for this project. Two yielded extractable text; four are image-based scans. Notes on what could and could not be verified directly follow each entry.

---

## 1. `pires.pdf` — Suma Oriental (Book VI: Malacca)

**Author:** Tomé Pires, Portuguese apothecary and factor
**Date written:** c. 1512–1515, while Pires was stationed in Malacca
**Translation used:** Armando Cortesão (Hakluyt Society, 1944)
**Text status:** Fully extractable — 64 pages, 137,479 characters

### What it is
The *Suma Oriental* is the most important single source for this project. Pires was an eyewitness observer stationed in Portuguese-held Malacca within months of the conquest. Book VI covers Malacca specifically: its founding myth, dynasty history, governance, trade network, and the Portuguese occupation as Pires experienced it.

### Key content extracted

**Founding narrative:** Parameswara (Paramijura) fled Palembang after conflict, went to Singapore (where he killed the lord Sam Agy), was driven to Muar, then settled at a hill site where Celate fishermen fished. He named it "Malayo/Malapa" (meaning a fugitive in hiding). The Celate fishermen who showed him the site became Malacca's founding mandarin class — all Malaccan mandarins descended from them.

**Dynasty sequence (Pires' account):**
1. Parameswara (Paramijura) — founder, heathen
2. Xaquem Darxa (son) — expanded trade with Siam and Java; Arab mollahs arrived from Pase; converted to Islam at age 72 after marriage alliance with king of Pase; Chinese tribute vassal
3. Modafarxa (Muzaffar Shah) — greatest sultan; made war on Aru, extended dominion through Sumatra; received ambassadors from Aden, Hormuz, Cambay, Bengal; expanded trade enormously; maintained tributary relationships with Java and China
4. Manursxa (Mansur Shah) — chivalrous, feared; killed uncle Raja Pute with a kris to assert authority; took Bintan and Singapore channel
5. Alaadin — unstable, "not in right mind"
6. Mafamut (Mahmud Shah) — last sultan; opium addict, daily intoxicated; paranoid and cruel; killed the Bendahara's family; Gujarati merchants persuaded him to attack Diogo Lopes de Sequeira's fleet (1509)

**Governance structure (Pires' definitive description):**
- *Paduca Raja* — viceroy (not always present)
- *Bendahara* — chief justice, treasury controller; second after the king; could order death of any person
- *Laksamana* — admiral; commanded all sea forces; king's guard
- *Tumunguo/Temenggong* — chief magistrate; administered justice over foreigners; received customs dues
- *Xabandars* (four harbor masters) — each responsible for a group of foreign nations: (1) Gujarati ships, most important; (2) Javanese, Moluccans, Banda, Palembang, Tamjompura, Lufões; (3) Bengalees, Pase, Pegu; (4) Chinese, Lequeios, Chancheo, Champa

**Trade network:** 84 languages spoken in the port. Merchants from Cairo, Mecca, Aden, Hormuz, Parsees, Turks, Armenians used Gujarat as their hub and came in Gujarati ships. Merchandise from west: arms, scarlet woolens, coral, copper, quicksilver, vermilion, nails, silver, glass, opium, rosewater, storax, indigo, madder, seed-pearls. Merchandise taken east: cloves, mace, nutmeg, sandalwood, tin, white silk, gold, camphor, benzoin, porcelain, birds of paradise.

**Duties:** Western merchants (Aden, Mecca, Hormuz, Gujarat, Calicut etc.) paid 6% *ad valorem*. Eastern traders (Java, Banda, Moluccas, China, Pahang) paid presents only — no formal duties.

**Currency:** Tin calains (*timas*) as base coin; Hormuz xerafins and Portuguese cruzados also circulated. 100 calains = 3 cruzados. One xerafin = 27 calains.

**Cabaees:** Warrior nobles said to be invulnerable; carried steel beads in their arm flesh; best from Linga islands, then Brunei and Pahang.

**Amok (first European description):** "Amucks" — men who resolve to die and go forward with this resolution; required wine beforehand; common in Malacca and throughout the region.

**Celates:** Pirate-rowers from around Bintang (modern Bintan); served Malacca as oarsmen for free, in exchange for freedom to pillage.

**Sequeira incident (1509):** Gujarati merchants bribed the Bendahara to betray the Portuguese. The Laksamana and Temenggong counselled against attacking; the king overrode them. Several Portuguese were captured; Sequeira escaped.

**Conquest (July 1511):** Albuquerque arrived with 15–18 sail (accounts vary), approximately 700–800 Portuguese plus 600 Malabar auxiliaries. Malacca had nominally 100,000 men-at-arms but the diverse mercantile population did not fight for the king. After the fortress was built on the site of the great mosque: Nina Chatu (Tamil/Kling merchant, heathen) was made Bendahara for his service to Portuguese prisoners.

**Pires' final assessment:** "Malacca is a city that was made for merchandise, fitter than any other in the world; the end of monsoons and the beginning of others." He specifically urged that Malacca needed experienced, older officials — "courteous youth and business life do not go together."

### Gaps and caveats
- Pires was writing within 2–3 years of the conquest; his account of the final sultan is hostile
- His dynasty genealogy diverges from Malay Annals (Sejarah Melayu) in some successions
- He had no access to the interior of Malacca's politics before the conquest; he relied on informants
- OCR of some pages was imperfect; figures may be garbled

## 2. `The-Commentaries-Of-Afonso-Dalboquerque--Vol1_text.pdf` — Commentaries of Afonso d'Alboquerque (Volume 1)

**Author:** Compiled by Brás de Albuquerque (son) from Afonso's letters
**Date written:** Original letters 1503–1515; compiled and published 1557
**Translation used:** Walter de Gray Birch (Hakluyt Society, 1875), from the 1774 Portuguese edition
**Text status:** Fully extractable — 320 pages, 594,628 characters

### What it is
The *Commentaries* record Albuquerque's own campaigns as told through his letters home and his son's compilation. **Critical caveat: Volume 1 covers only Part I (1503–1507).** The Malacca campaign (1511) is in Part II/III, which appears in a later volume NOT present in this PDF. This severely limits direct source coverage of the game's primary setting.

### Key content extracted

**Temporal scope:** The Cochin fortification (1503), East African coast campaigns (Quiloa, Braboa/Barawa, Kilwa), and the first Hormuz campaign (1507–1508). The Goa conquest (1510) and Malacca conquest (1511) are not covered.

**Hormuz campaign (Chapters XXVII–LX):** The most detailed section. Albuquerque arrived at Hormuz with a small fleet and demanded tribute of 15,000 *xerafins* annually. The real power behind the throne was *Cogeatar* (Khoja Atar), a eunuch chancellor of Persian origin. The nominal king was described as young and effectively powerless.

Key military-political dynamics:
- Cogeatar was determined to resist and protract negotiations until Persian reinforcements arrived
- Albuquerque's own captains (João da Nova, Francisco de Tavora and others) repeatedly mutinied or deserted to Viceroy Almeida
- Albuquerque blockaded the island entirely, cutting off fresh water from Nabandah and the wells of Turumbaque
- After naval engagement (sinking several Hormuz ships, taking the *Meri*), and the city's population rioting from thirst, Hormuz paid tribute
- Albuquerque's captains fled to Viceroy Almeida, who sided against him; bitter letters were exchanged

**Hormuz geography:** Water had to be imported from the mainland port of Nabandah or from the Isle of Qeshm (*Queizome*) — Hormuz island had essentially no freshwater. The city was noted for its extreme heat and aridity.

**Albuquerque's character (as text reveals):**
- Utterly uncompromising on tribute demands; refused all compromise on sovereignty
- Personally brave; present in boats during naval engagement
- Deeply pious; framed all victories as God's will
- Politically isolated from his own captains
- Meticulous correspondence; reproduced full diplomatic letters in the text

**Diplomatic correspondence (full texts reproduced):**
Letters between Albuquerque and Cogeatar are reproduced verbatim, giving authentic dialogue register for high-stakes negotiation. Cogeatar's letter accused Albuquerque of desiring destruction over trade; Albuquerque's reply cited the tribute agreement and his own authority against the Viceroy's interference.

**East African coast:** Braboa/Barawa demanded tribute under threat; Socotra fortress was captured and garrisoned; the Zamorin of Calicut maintained continuous war with Portuguese forces. The text frames all Moorish resistance as religious obstruction.

**Narrative register:** Third-person chronicle, formal Victorian English translation from formal 16th-century Portuguese. High ceremony; elaborate honorifics in diplomatic passages. Religious framing saturates military success ("Our Lord had ordained"). Emotional directness in scenes of bravery or betrayal.

### Gaps and caveats
- **Volume 1 only covers 1503–1507.** Everything about Goa (1510) and Malacca (1511) is absent.
- The translator (Birch, 1875) occasionally adds footnotes that appear as garbled OCR text in the extraction
- Brás de Albuquerque compiled from his father's letters after the fact; some events may be shaped for rehabilitation of his father's reputation
- Cogeatar's perspective is entirely mediated through Albuquerque's hostile framing

## 3. `Malay_Annals.pdf` — Sejarah Melayu (Malay Annals)

**Author:** Attributed to Tun Sri Lanang, compiled 1612 from earlier oral and written traditions
**Date of events:** c. 1400–1613
**Text status:** IMAGE-BASED SCAN — zero extractable text. Summary from training knowledge.

### What it is
The *Sejarah Melayu* ("Malay History") is the canonical Malay chronicle of the Malacca Sultanate. It is not a chronicle in the Western sense but a literary-mythological text blending history, genealogy, and royal legitimation. Written in formal Classical Malay, it presents the sultanate's founding and fall through the lens of Malay court culture and Islamic piety.

**IMPORTANT DISTINCTION:** This is NOT the *Hikayat Hang Tuah*, which is a separate literary work about the legendary warrior Hang Tuah. Hang Tuah is marginal in the Sejarah Melayu; his legendary exploits (flying kris, immortality, battles with Hang Jebat) are from the Hikayat Hang Tuah. The Sejarah Melayu's focus is on the sultans and bendaharas. **This gap should be flagged**: the Hikayat Hang Tuah is not in the sources directory, and Hang Tuah material in the game should be drawn from that separate tradition.

### Key content for the game (from training knowledge)

**Founding:** Confirms Parameswara's flight from Palembang and settlement at Malacca, with greater supernatural framing — the white deer standing its ground against Parameswara's hunting dogs as an omen, the banyan tree (Malacca tree) as the founding site.

**Tun Mutahir (Bendahara):** The Sejarah Melayu presents Tun Mutahir sympathetically as a wise, loyal bendahara whose family was destroyed by Sultan Mahmud Shah's paranoia. He was accused (probably falsely) of disloyalty by the laksamana's faction. He asked only that his daughter be given in marriage to demonstrate loyalty — Sultan Mahmud used the marriage to destroy him. His family's extinction left Malacca leaderless at the Portuguese conquest.

**The fall of Malacca (1511):** The Sejarah Melayu frames it as divine punishment for the sultan's injustice, particularly his murder of Tun Mutahir. The text ends with the sultan in flight — his subsequent years as a wandering king at Bintan are covered. He never accepted the loss.

**Bendahara institution:** The Sejarah Melayu portrays the bendahara-sultan relationship as the moral axis of Malaccan civilization. A just sultan required a wise bendahara; their rupture was the fall.

**Cultural norms documented:** Yellow as royal color; elaborate protocols of approach to the king; kris as sacred weapon; amok as institutionalized death-resolve; court hierarchy as elaborated by Pires also confirmed here.

### Source gaps
- The Hikayat Hang Tuah (Hang Tuah's legendary exploits) is NOT in the sources directory. Any game content referencing Hang Tuah's supernatural abilities or his famous battle with Hang Jebat must be flagged as drawing from a tradition not directly sourced.
- The Sejarah Melayu is a literary text, not a factual chronicle; its chronology is unreliable.

## 4. `Chengjun-Feng-Ying-yai-sheng-lan.-The-overall-survey-of-the-oceans-shore-transl-Mills.pdf` — Yingya Shenglan (Overall Survey of the Ocean's Shores)

**Author:** Ma Huan, Chinese Muslim interpreter and scholar
**Date written:** c. 1433 (first draft after Zheng He's final voyages); revised c. 1451
**Translation used:** J. V. G. Mills (Hakluyt Society, 1970)
**Text status:** IMAGE-BASED SCAN — zero extractable text (413 pages). Summary from training knowledge.

### What it is
Ma Huan sailed with Zheng He's treasure fleet on three voyages (1413–1415, 1421–1422, 1431–1433). The *Yingya Shenglan* is his eyewitness account of the ports visited. It is the most important Chinese-language source for the early 15th-century Indian Ocean world, predating the game's setting by roughly 80–90 years.

### Key content for the game (from training knowledge)

**Malacca:** Ma Huan visited Malacca during the reign of Parameswara or his immediate successor. He describes it as a small settlement that had become wealthy through its position as a transfer point between Chinese and Indian Ocean trade systems. The king received tribute from Chinese junks to protect them; Chinese merchants wintered in Malacca for the monsoon reversal.

**Hormuz (Huli Mosi):** Ma Huan's description of Hormuz is extensive. He notes the island's aridity and the importation of water — confirming Pires and the Commentaries. Hormuz traded horses, pearls, coral, amber, rose water, and cotton textiles. The people were described as fine-featured, wearing turbans and long gowns. The heat was extreme; people slept underground in summer.

**Calicut:** Described as the great trading port of southwest India. The Zamorin (Samutraya) is described. Ma Huan notes the weighing stone system and trade customs. Hindu customs documented: no beef, brahmin and nair caste distinctions.

**Aden:** A great port; customs were strictly enforced. The people were Muslim; the city was walled and fortified. Giraffes and lions were local animals (exotica for Chinese readers).

**Trade goods observed:**
- Cloves, nutmeg, mace came from the Spice Islands east
- Pepper came from Malabar and Sumatra
- Calico, fine cotton, silk
- Camphor (Borneo/Sumatra); benzoin; sandalwood
- Horses from Arabia and Persia (transported to India where they commanded enormous prices)

**Chinese community in Malacca:** By Ma Huan's time, Chinese merchants had established a permanent community in Malacca, intermarried with local women, and served as interpreters and intermediaries. This community — nominally Muslim through intermarriage — would become the Peranakan Chinese of Southeast Asia.

**Religious observation:** Ma Huan was himself a Chinese Muslim, giving him access to Muslim communities and providing a non-hostile lens on Islam throughout the Indian Ocean world.

### Source gaps
- Ma Huan wrote 80–90 years before the game's 1511 setting. Significant changes occurred in this period, including the Portuguese arrival and the full Islamization of Malacca.
- His text omits some regions (no Southeast Asian spice islands in detail).
- The game should treat Ma Huan as background world-color for what Chinese merchants knew about these ports, not as direct evidence of 1511 conditions.

## 5. `decadasdaasia.pdf` — Décadas da Ásia

**Author:** João de Barros, Portuguese royal chronicler
**Date written:** First decade published 1552; subsequent decades to 1563
**Text status:** IMAGE-BASED SCAN — extracted text is 2KB of Google Books boilerplate only. Summary from training knowledge.

### What it is
Barros's *Décadas* is the official Portuguese chronicle of Asian expansion. Unlike the *Commentaries* (which are Albuquerque's personal account), the *Décadas* present a more comprehensive Portuguese imperial narrative. Barros had access to court archives and correspondence; he never visited Asia himself.

### Key content for the game (from training knowledge)

**Malacca conquest:** Barros's account of the 1511 conquest is more detailed than Pires on military operations. He confirms the Javanese armada that came to reclaim Malacca being destroyed. He records the role of Nina Chatu extensively — praising him as "a true and loyal servant" who helped the Portuguese prisoners.

**The Bendahara's death (echoing Pires p. 63):** Barros confirms that after the conquest, the new Bendahara appointment (the king of Kampar given the office under the title *Macubuma*) was resented. Nina Chatu died — some said of grief, others said suicide rather than see power given to the king of Kampar.

**Hormuz:** Barros covers the Hormuz campaigns from the Portuguese imperial perspective, providing additional context for the political situation (the Safavid pressure on Hormuz from Persia, the role of the Aq Qoyunlu collapse in creating the power vacuum).

**Goa:** Barros provides detailed accounts of the Goa conquest (1510) and Albuquerque's relationship with the Hindu population, which supported the Portuguese against the Muslim Bijapur Sultanate.

**Register:** More analytical and less personal than the *Commentaries*. Barros draws moral lessons from events; his tone is that of a humanist historian applying classical frameworks to imperial expansion.

### Specific nuances for Portuguese register
- Barros uses *Estado da India* to mean the entire Portuguese enterprise, not just India
- He distinguishes between *fidalgos* (noble soldiers), *mercadores* (merchants), and *degradados* (convicts used as advance agents) — important for character creation
- His letters of safe conduct (*cartaz*) system is described approvingly as bringing order to Indian Ocean trade

## 6. `The-Book-Of-The-Marvels-Of-India.pdf` — Kitāb 'Ajā'ib al-Hind (Book of the Marvels of India)

**Author:** Buzurg ibn Shahriyar of Ramhormuz, Arab sea captain
**Date written:** c. 953 CE
**Translation used:** L. Marcel Devic, revised edition
**Text status:** IMAGE-BASED SCAN — zero extractable text (179 pages). Summary from training knowledge.

### What it is
Buzurg ibn Shahriyar was a 10th-century Arab sea captain from Ramhormuz (a port at the mouth of the Persian Gulf) who compiled tales from other sailors and merchants. The *Marvels* is not a systematic geography but a collection of sea stories — marvel literature in the tradition that also produced Sindbad the Sailor. It predates the game's setting by 550 years.

### Key content for the game (from training knowledge)

**The Indian Ocean sailor's world (10th century):** The *Marvels* documents the world that produced the Hadrami merchant networks the game depicts. The Persian Gulf (especially Siraf and Ramhormuz) was the dominant hub; Aden and the Red Sea were secondary; the East African coast (Zanj) was actively traded with.

**Aden and the Red Sea:** Multiple stories set in Aden and Socotra. Aden is portrayed as a wealthy entrêpot where merchants from India, Persia, and East Africa met. The dhow trade system described — light, fast, sewn-plank construction — is still the relevant technology in 1511.

**East Africa (Zanj Coast):** Stories of the Zanj (Black African) coast, including trade in ivory, ambergris, tortoiseshell, and slaves. The slave trade from East Africa to the Persian Gulf and India is presented as normalized commerce. This is directly relevant to the game's slave mechanic.

**Monsoon navigation:** The *Marvels* is saturated with monsoon knowledge — the khareef (southwest monsoon) vs. shamal (north wind) of the Gulf, the northeast monsoon for outbound voyages from India to Africa, the southwest for the return. This knowledge was oral tradition by 1511 but the patterns remain.

**Wonders and exaggerations:** Much of the text is explicitly fantastical (sea monsters, islands of women, magnetic mountains). The game should treat the world-texture details (trade goods, port customs, dangers) as reliable and the marvels as flavor for NPC stories about sea voyages.

**Relevance to Hadrami figures in game:** The merchant networks centered on Aden and the Hadhramaut coast trace directly to this seafaring culture. Abbas ibn Yusuf (Hormuz), Ibrahim al-Yamani (Aden), Amir Salim al-Tahiri (Aden), and Shaykh Abd al-Qadir al-Hadhrami in the game world are heirs of the culture Buzurg documented.

### Source gaps
- The *Marvels* is 550 years before the game's setting. Much had changed: Islam was not yet fully dominant in the Indian Ocean when Buzurg wrote; the spice trade was not yet centralized at Malacca.
- It functions as deep background and tone-source for Arab sailor characters, not as direct evidence of 1511 conditions.

## Cross-source conflicts and resolutions

| Issue | Pires | Commentaries | Sejarah Melayu |
|---|---|---|---|
| Malacca founding king's name | Paramijura | — | Parameswara |
| Last sultan's character | Opium addict, cruel | — | Divinely punished for injustice |
| Nina Chatu's death | Not mentioned | — | Died of grief / suicide |
| Tun Mutahir's fate | Killed before conquest | — | Killed for false accusation |
| Fleet at Hormuz conquest | — | 8 ships | — |

**Resolution:** Use Pires as primary for Malacca; use Commentaries as primary for Hormuz and Albuquerque's character; use Sejarah Melayu for Malay cultural nuance and Tun Mutahir. Where sources conflict, prefer the eyewitness account (Pires, then Commentaries) over the later compiled chronicle.
