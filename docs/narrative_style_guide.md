# Narrative Style Guide — The Straits Project

Tone, voice, vocabulary, and dialogue register for all text in the game. This is the canonical guide. All new events, quest text, NPC dialogue, and lore fragments must conform to these standards.

---

## Core Tone

**What this game sounds like:** A merchant captain's log that has become a story. Specific, sensory, material. The things that matter are the price of pepper, the weight of artillery, the smell of rain on a teak deck, the color of the Zamorin's court. Abstract moralizing is almost never voiced. Characters think in concrete terms — cargo, loyalty, weather, debt.

**What it is NOT:**
- Swashbuckling adventure (no jolly Roger energy, no treasure-map language)
- Dry academic history (no passive voice, no "it is recorded that")
- Fantasy-inflected (no anachronistic concepts; no genre-coded language like "dark magic" or "ancient evil")
- Modern-framed (no contemporary political framing bleeding through)

**The register:** Literate but not ornate. The sources (Pires, Albuquerque) write with directness and occasional bluntness. Albuquerque's letters are frank, even crude in places. Pires catalogs with the precision of an apothecary. Ma Huan observes with Chinese court formality. The Sejarah Melayu is courtly and formal when describing royalty, matter-of-fact when describing violence. Buzurg ibn Shahriyar is a storyteller who trusts his listener.

## Register by Character Type

### Portuguese (Tomé de Faro, Camila de Sousa, Rui Barbosa, Estêvão da Guiné, Diogo, Simão)

**Baseline:** Practical; action-focused; Catholic framing is present but worn, not devout. Distance from Portugal creates a kind of philosophical bluntness. Reference to God and Christ's Passion is routine filler in formal statements, not heartfelt declaration.

**Vocabulary:** Use Portuguese words for titles and ranks: *capitão*, *feitor* (factor/trade agent), *fidalgo* (nobleman), *degredado* (convicted criminal exiled to serve as spy/envoy), *armada*, *cartaz* (protection pass), *xerafin* (coin), *cruzado* (coin), *bahar* (weight unit, ~220–225 kg), *creese* (Malay dagger — Portuguese transliteration). Do not use modern English equivalents where the Portuguese term is the correct word.

**Dialect notes:** Characters speak modern English in the text, but period-Portuguese flavor comes through in sentence structure: direct statements first, qualifications after. No hedging before the point.

**Sample register (Rui Barbosa, experienced navigator):**
> "Wind holds from the northeast. Three days to the strait, maybe four if we're unlucky. Buy your cartaz at Malacca or they'll board us in the channel — and I'm not spending another week explaining myself to some twenty-year-old capitão with a new sword."

**Sample register (Tomé de Faro, formal):**
> "I told the factor that the cargo was declared. He told me the captain-general had different information. We paid the difference and said nothing. That's how it's done here."

### Ottoman (Mehmed al-Rumi, Kemal)

**Baseline:** Educated; cosmopolitan; speaks multiple languages as a matter of professional survival. Ottoman Turkish, Arabic, and some Persian in vocabulary. The Ottoman merchant class of 1511 is sophisticated, well-traveled, and politically aware. They know the Mamluk empire is weakening and watch Istanbul's moves carefully.

**Vocabulary:** *nakhoda* (ship captain — Arabic/Persian nautical term used across the Indian Ocean); *diwan* (court/council); *bey* (lord, rank); *pasha* (higher rank, Year 6+); *qadi* (judge); *baqqal* (merchant); *dirham*, *dinar*, *ashrafi* (coins); *suq* (market). Do not use "bazaar" (anachronistic feel) — use *suq* or just "market."

**Tone:** Mehmed al-Rumi speaks in the voice of a man who has navigated courts from Istanbul to Aden. He is precise about hierarchy. He gives compliments with calculation. He does not volunteer information; he offers it in exchange.

**Sample register (Mehmed, diplomatic):**
> "The Zamorin's man wanted to know where we sailed from. I told him Aden, which is true enough. He did not ask what we carried, which told me he already knew, or that he didn't care, which would be worse."

**Sample register (Kemal, blunt):**
> "Don't argue with the harbor fees here. Pay what they ask, write it in your ledger as a gift to God, and be done with it."

### Chinese (Chen Mingzhi, Old Liang, Ah Kow, Wei Chongde)

**Baseline:** Formal hierarchy; older men address younger men with the authority of long experience. Face (reputation/honor) governs behavior — a public insult is a crisis; a private slight is negotiable. Business is conducted with ritual social exchange before the transaction itself. Chinese merchants are not warm to strangers but are reliably transactional.

**Vocabulary:** *junk* (Chinese ocean-going vessel); *nakhoda* used by other cultures for Chinese captains but Chinese characters may use their own term; *tael* (silver weight as currency); *li* (distance unit); surnames come first (Chen Mingzhi = Family Chen, given name Mingzhi). Avoid romanization clutter — do not put Wade-Giles transcriptions in dialogue. Simple, clear English with formal register.

**Tone:** Chen Mingzhi speaks with the caution of a man who has seen empires change and knows the Chinese community's position in Malacca is precarious. Old Liang speaks with the authority of someone who sailed with older fleets and has no illusions left. Ah Kow speaks practically, with dry humor. Wei Chongde speaks carefully, always holding something back.

**Sample register (Chen Mingzhi, guarded):**
> "We have traded in this port for sixty years. The Portuguese are new. We will be here after the Portuguese are gone. This does not mean I am their enemy. It means I am careful."

**Sample register (Old Liang, memory):**
> "When Cheng Ho's ships came through this strait, the water was thick with them. Seventy, eighty ships. The king came down to the shore himself. I was twelve years old. I remember the sound."

### Arab/Swahili (Yusuf al-Halabi, Baraka)

**Baseline:** Yusuf al-Halabi is a long-distance Levantine Arab trader who knows the Mediterranean world and the Indian Ocean world equally. He thinks in networks — who knows whom, who owes whom. Baraka is Swahili-East African; his register reflects a world where the Indian Ocean is not an obstacle but a road, and Islamic practice is woven into daily rhythm.

**Vocabulary:** *dhow* (Arab/Swahili vessel — lateen sails, flexible hull); *nakhoda*; *al-hindi* (of India); *al-zanj* (East African, from the Swahili coast); *dinar*, *dirham*; *inshallah* (if God wills — natural speech, not exotic decoration); *bismillah* (in the name of God — opening of action); *suq*; *shaykh* (elder/respected man). Swahili words: *bwana* (sir, master), *asante* (thanks) — use sparingly when Baraka is in a context where Swahili would be natural.

**Tone:** Yusuf is dry, commercial, careful with loyalty. Baraka is warmer and more direct; his Islamic faith is practiced, not performed. When Baraka frees slaves, it is because his faith makes it obvious, not because he is making a statement.

**Sample register (Baraka, on freeing a slave):**
> "It is written that freeing a slave is the ransom of a soul. I did not need to think about it. Neither should you."

**Sample register (Yusuf, commercial):**
> "I'm not saying the price is wrong. I'm saying the price is what it is now, and next month it will be different, and the man who waits has the advantage over the man who hurries."

## Vocabulary Reference

**Period-accurate terms to use:**
- *bahar* — unit of weight (~220–225 kg); used for bulk commodities (pepper, rice)
- *xerafin* — Portuguese gold coin; used in Indian Ocean trade; Ormuz tribute was in xerafins
- *cruzado* — Portuguese silver/gold coin
- *dirham/dinar* — Arab currency; use for Arab/Ottoman characters
- *tael* — Chinese silver weight; use for Chinese characters
- *cartaz* — Portuguese protection pass; all ships must have one or risk seizure
- *feitor* — Portuguese factor (trade agent stationed at a port)
- *degredado* — Portuguese convict/exile used as spy, envoy, or pioneer agent
- *fidalgo* — Portuguese nobleman (may be impoverished); important for social register
- *nakhoda* — captain of a merchant vessel; used across Arab, Persian, Malay, Indian cultures
- *creese/kris* — Malay poisoned dagger; the weapon of Malay culture
- *suq* — market (Arab); use instead of "bazaar"
- *dhow* — Arab/Swahili lateen-sail vessel
- *junk* — Chinese ocean-going vessel
- *sampan* — small river/harbor boat
- *proa/prau* — Malay vessel; smaller than a junk
- *bombard* — cannon (Portuguese/period-accurate; "cannon" is also acceptable)
- *Zamorin* — title of the ruler of Calicut (not a personal name)
- *Bendahara* — Malay title; treasury official and chief minister
- *Lassamane* — Malay title; admiral
- *Xabandar/Syahbandar* — harbor master; also overseer of merchant community groups
- *Orang Kaya* — Malay title; "rich man" = wealthy merchant-noble
- *Temenggung* — Malay title; commander/chief of police

**Modern terms to avoid in period text:**
- "negotiate" → use "parley," "treat with," "come to terms with"
- "strategy" → use "counsel," "plan," "design"
- "economy" → use "trade," "commerce," "the market"
- "culture" → use "custom," "manner," "way of living"
- "propaganda" → use "report," "rumor," "false account"
- "politics" → use "affairs," "the court," "the great men's quarrels"
- "religion" → context-specific: "the Faith," "the true religion," "their law"

## Event Text Standards

**Length:** 80–200 words for event description. 20–50 words per option. Lore fragments 60–120 words.

**Structure of event description:**
1. Sensory grounding (what the player sees/hears/smells — one sentence)
2. The situation (two to three sentences of concrete detail)
3. The choice framing (what requires a decision) — do NOT summarize the options

**Example (well-formed):**
> The harbor master's man blocks the gangway with his body and two soldiers at his back. He holds a list — the cartaz records, by the look of it — and his expression says he has found something he likes.
>
> "Your captain's name does not match the feitor's register from last season. There is a discrepancy."
>
> He is smiling now. He likes the discrepancy.

**Example (poorly formed — avoid):**
> A harbor official approaches you. He seems to be questioning your cartaz. You must decide how to handle this situation carefully.

## Lore Fragment Standards

Lore fragments are short pieces of history, legend, or rumor that appear in taverns, from NPCs, or as sea travel events. They require no player choice — they are the game's world breathing.

**Sources to draw from:**
- Marvels of India: sea creatures, island legends, distant sea tales
- Sejarah Melayu: Malaccan founding myths, Badang's strength, Hang Tuah's charisma, were-tigers
- Ma Huan: Chinese perspective on Malacca before the conquest; crocodiles; Cheng Ho gifts
- Pires/Commentaries: specific trade facts delivered as overheard conversation

**Format:** Present tense or reported speech. No named narrator unless it's a specific character. Short — one paragraph.

**Example:**
> There is a story the old Malay fishermen tell about the bridge. Before the Portuguese came, twenty pavilions stood over the water, and the merchants of eighty nations bought and sold there in the same morning. The Malay for "eighty languages" is the same word as "eighty markets." The Portuguese say this is an exaggeration. The fishermen say: count the empty stalls.

## Translation Convention (_es keys)

All player-facing text must have a `_es` parallel key. Latin American Spanish, neutral register — not Castilian Spanish.

**Rules for _es text:**
- Translate meaning, not word-for-word
- Period vocabulary: use Spanish equivalents where they exist (*capitán*, *factor*, *xerafín*, *creís* [creese], *patrón* [ship captain])
- The same tone rules apply: direct, sensory, specific
- Do not over-explain in the _es version — trust the reader
- Malay/Arabic/Chinese terms: keep unchanged in both versions (they are proper nouns of the period world)
